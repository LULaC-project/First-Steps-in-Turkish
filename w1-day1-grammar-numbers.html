Less is More! — Meet the Simit
🥯
🛒
Simit Arabası · Street Cart
Simit — the ultimate Turkish street snack. A circular, sesame-crusted bread ring sold by street vendors (simitçi) across Turkey. In Istanbul alone, an estimated 300,000 simits are sold every day. Often eaten for breakfast with white cheese and tea. ☕
1 simit ✓
5 simit ✓
5 simitler ✗
💡 A number already means "more than one"!
2
Teacher's Audio Script
🎙 Audio
As you walk through the city, you'll smell something amazing. That's the simit! It's the king of Turkish street food — a crunchy, sesame-crusted bread ring that every local loves.

Now, imagine you're at a red simit cart and you want to buy five of them for your friends. Here's a city tip: Turkish people love efficiency. In English, you'd say "Five simits". But in Turkish, the number beş (five) already tells us there's more than one. So if you already have a number, you don't use the plural ending. We just say: Beş simit. No extra plural piece needed!

It's cleaner, faster, and perfectly logical. Why say more when the number says it all? One simit, five simit, a hundred simit — the word stays the same when you count! This is something beginners often forget, so keep an eye on it.
3
The Rule at a Glance
✅ DO — with a number
Beş simit
İki taksi
On mağaza
When a number precedes the noun, keep the noun in its singular form. The number already signals plurality.
❌ DON'T — double plural
Beş simitler
İki taksiler
On mağazalar
Do not add -lar / -ler after a number. The number + suffix combo is redundant in Turkish.
No number? Use the plural suffix -lar / -ler.  |  Number present? Keep the noun singular!
